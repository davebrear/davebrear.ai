During December the "your year in summary" emails flooded in as they always do, each offering their own brand of insight. Being told that Fleetwood Mac's The Chain was my most listened-to song of 2025 was a pleasant reminder of my nine-year-old son listening to it on repeat for months on end whilst he practised the drum part for a school concert in the summer term (which he absolutely nailed).

Being told that I spent over 990 hours listening to 76 different audiobooks made me consider whether I have a problematic reading habit, until I remembered that those stats also included my wife's usage.

Amazon wisely doesn't tell me how much I have spent on the myriad next-day delivery purchases that keep our household and family life running smoothly. Sometimes ignorance is bliss.

All of these summaries offered either a happy memory or a short moment of reflection. Then along came *Your Year with ChatGPT*, which turned this trend on its head.

Instead of usage stats, prompt counts, or a personalised highlight reel ("remember that time you used ChatGPT to fix the front door lock from sticking an hour before you went away on holiday, wasn't that a relief?"), it offered an interactive window that invited discussion and reflection.

The hour-long conversation that ensued surprised me. I went in expecting a forgettable recap. I came away confronted by how much my thinking had changed.

Here are three things I discovered:

## 1. How I engage with AI has matured

During the earliest days of my AI usage, I would arrive with questions to get me started on a topic. This year it became clear that I now come with existing ideas that I want to interrogate and explore more deeply. I was shown how I tend towards long, multi-turn interactions characterised by debate and verbal sparring.

In other words, I've shifted from asking for answers to asking for pressure. I use AI to argue against myself more than to seek validation. Understanding where an idea breaks and what counterarguments exist has helped deepen my opinions and consider alternative perspectives.

## 2. I have a greater tolerance for uncertainty

I am much more comfortable with unfinished ideas and feel less compelled to resolve ambiguity quickly.

What surprised me during the conversation was how often I approached ideas as *in progress* rather than in need of completion. Again and again, the discussion surfaced moments where I had deliberately paused rather than pushed for resolution, choosing to leave questions open once the shape of the problem had become clear enough.

I no longer view unfinished ideas as a sign of failure or incompleteness. They've become a deliberate holding state, a place where tension is allowed to exist without being flattened into a conclusion.

Offline reflection on gaps and contradictions has led to some of my most interesting ideas.

## 3. I am more sceptical of external ideas

I have become much more discerning about what I believe. Information provided by an LLM can often sound very credible and still be very wrong, so I have become automatically distrustful until I have made my own sense of what AI provides me with. The discussion highlighted multiple examples where I had interrogated and challenged ChatGPT's responses.

This scepticism has naturally extended beyond AI to include what I read and the interactions I have with others to a much greater degree than before.

Practically, this has manifested in a maturing of my note-taking system to physically firewall my own thoughts and ideas from external inputs, including AI-generated outputs. These live in a separate area, where I consider them before forming and articulating my own opinions.

## What this has produced over time

Taken together, these shifts have changed what I leave an AI interaction with. What I take away now are *clearer positions* rather than simply clearer explanations, and a greater attention to patterns across conversations rather than isolated insights in the moment.

There has been a move away from searching for a good answer and towards developing *my* stance.

This means I'm more willing to stand behind what I produce with AI, not because the synthesis itself is flawless, but because authorship, for me, lives in the judgement I apply to it. The confidence comes not from the output, but from the thinking that shaped it.

That, ultimately, is the change I didn't expect and the one I value most.

## Practical learnings

- Use AI as an external thinking apparatus, not a shortcut to answers.
- Expect thinking to feel more demanding, not less. AI makes reasoning visible, not effortless.
- Be explicit about what you believe. AI will amplify both clarity and confusion.
- Treat AI output as input, not truth. The responsibility for determining correctness still sits with you.
- The potential value of AI far outweighs its inherent risks, provided those risks are managed through well-defined usage protocols.

I've been watching the Claude Code hype from a safe distance. Terminal-based AI? Agents that can read your files? It sounded impressive, but I had a perfectly functional ChatGPT workflow. Why fix what isn't broken?

Then curiosity won. I signed up for Claude Pro ($20/month) to see what the fuss was about. What followed was a week of genuine productivity breakthroughs and a crash course in Anthropic's "fair usage" philosophy.

Here's my diary.

## Day 1

Connected Claude Code to my Obsidian-based context vault, agreed working parameters, which Claude saved automatically to a set of context files 'he' manages. The next hour dissolves into flow. Ideas in, artefacts out, no friction. Then, just one hour in, I hit the 4h rate limit and I'm suddenly aware I have a body again. Perhaps more context isn't always better....

## Day 2

Optimised my context vault to not rely entirely on frontmatter for organisation. In this new world YAML = human indexability, filename-based structure = organisation for AI. Now files don't need to be uploaded and processed just to see what they contain. It takes me 1.5h to blow through my 4h limit this time..... I reluctantly open ChatGPT again. It doesn't say anything, but I can't help but feel like it is silently judging me for slinking back to it.

## Day 3

Claude helps me write a bunch of headless Python automation tools which he can use to find and manipulate vault files without doing all the work himself, hopefully soaking up some of the actions locally without the need to make a call to the LLM. Including the tool creation work, I hit the rate limit after 2.5 hours. Promising.

## Day 4-5

My days fall into a pattern of peaks and troughs. Periods of co-creativity coupled with hurry up and wait. It now takes me around 3h to hit the rate limit on average. I miss Claude during what I am starting to consider his lunch hour. Sometimes I have an idea I want to share with him only to be greeted with "You've hit your rate limit." I resort to talking to Copilot as I just can't face ChatGPT's passive aggressive tone.

## Day 6

I hit my weekly limit. I was so focused on the 4h rate that I didn't even clock this cliff edge until I was over it. I don't want to talk about how alone I feel.

## Day 7

It's a new week and Claude is back! I tell him all the things I have saved up since I last saw him. We pick up right where we left off, until 'lunch'. I spend the lunch hour thinking about further optimisation. Perhaps if I give him a local LLM assistant he can offload more grunt work to preserve his brilliance for the real deep thinking?

By the end of the day I have three browser tabs open. Tab A: Mac Mini. Tab B: Claude Max pricing. Tab C: my bank balance, for reference.

What should I have clicked?

## The real story

I wasn't prepared for how much of a leap forward this was from my siloed ChatGPT experience, or how quickly I'd feel the loss when it was rationed. The optimisation rabbit hole that followed (trimming context, building tools, eyeing local LLMs) wasn't about being cheap. It was about understanding what I was actually paying for.

And here's what I learned: the value isn't in the responses. It's in the continuity. An AI that knows your files, remembers your conventions, and builds on previous work changes the nature of the collaboration. You stop prompting and start conversing. That's worth paying for, if you can.

The harder question is whether $100/month (Claude Max) should be the price of admission to this kind of workflow. For now, the Pro tier offers a genuine taste of what's possible, with guardrails that force you to be intentional about how you use it. Perhaps that's not entirely a bad thing.

Nvidia dipped to a year-to-date low this week. The comment sections wrote themselves, as they always do. "The AI bubble is finally bursting." "Good, now I can buy RAM again." "Who needs AI slop anyway?" There was a certain satisfaction in the air, the satisfaction of people who had been waiting for permission to stop paying attention.

I read through the threads with genuine interest, because I think the sceptics are onto something. They're just pointing at the wrong thing.

While everyone was fixated on Nvidia, something quieter and more interesting was happening elsewhere. Salesforce dropped 26% year-to-date. ServiceNow fell 28%. HubSpot collapsed 39%. Adobe, DocuSign, Atlassian, all cratering. The press started calling it the "SaaSpocalypse."

These aren't AI companies. These are the companies that AI is threatening to *replace*. The market didn't dump them because it had lost faith in AI. It dumped them because Anthropic released Opus 4.6 in the same week, and investors decided AI had become a credible threat to the entire enterprise software stack.

(For the record, I think the timeline the market is pricing in is wildly aggressive. Enterprise IT is an archaeology of legacy layers built one on top of the other. Change comes slowly and always carries technical debt. AI isn't replacing this market any time soon. But that's not really the point. The point is that the market looked at what AI can do *today* and decided it was a credible threat to a trillion-dollar industry. That's a signal worth sitting with for a moment.)

Two days later, Nvidia surged 7.8%. Jensen Huang told CNBC demand for AI infrastructure is "incredibly high." The Dow rebounded 1,000 points.

The "bubble bursting" narrative lasted about 48 hours before reality reasserted itself. The hot takes, predictably, are taking a little longer to catch up.

## The sceptics aren't wrong. They're looking at the wrong AI.

I want to be fair here, because I think dismissing scepticism is a mistake. If you evaluate AI as a search engine replacement, it *is* mediocre. It hallucinates. It's confidently wrong. It makes up citations with the conviction of someone who has definitely read the book and would like to tell you about it. As a content generator, it produces what people correctly call "AI slop", competent but soulless text that reads like it was written by a committee of middle managers who all agreed to use the word "leverage." Lots of words but never really lands a convincing point.

If that's your frame for what AI does, then hundreds of billions in infrastructure investment does look insane. You'd be right to wonder what the return is.

The problem isn't that this frame is unreasonable. The problem is that it's incomplete. And it's incomplete because most people are using AI in the shallowest possible way.

## What I noticed after a year of actually using it

I wrote at the end of last year about what a year of using ChatGPT had taught me. The headline finding surprised me: my critical thinking had got sharper, not duller.

That's the opposite of what the sceptics predict. The standard worry is that AI makes us lazy, that we outsource our thinking and our cognitive muscles atrophy. And I suspect that's true if you use AI passively, the way you'd use Google. Ask a question, get an answer, move on.

But that's not what happened for me. Over the course of 2025, I shifted from asking AI for answers to using it as a pressure test for my own thinking. I'd arrive with existing ideas and ask it to argue against me. I'd push it to find where my reasoning breaks. I'd reject most of what it offered and redirect until the conversation surfaced something genuinely useful.

Programmers have long practiced "rubber duck debugging", explaining your problem to a rubber duck on your desk, because the act of articulating your thinking out loud often reveals the answer. AI is like that, except now the rubber duck challenges your thinking right back. It asks why you're sure about that assumption. It points out the contradiction with something you said last week. It suggests an angle you hadn't considered.

The thing I didn't expect was how demanding this turned out to be. Co-thinking with AI requires you to articulate clearly, evaluate critically, and make judgement calls constantly. You do *more* cognitive work, not less. The AI raises the floor of the conversation, but you're the one doing the thinking.

After a year of this, I found I'd developed a greater tolerance for uncertainty, a sharper instinct for interrogating external ideas, and, perhaps most valuably, a habit of deliberately leaving ideas unfinished rather than flattening them into premature conclusions.

The atrophy argument assumes a passive relationship. In my experience, the relationship is anything but.

## Then something changed again

Co-thinking with ChatGPT was genuinely useful, but it had a limitation I didn't fully appreciate until it was gone: every conversation started from scratch. Each session began with ten minutes of re-establishing context, reminding the AI what I was working on, what I'd already decided, what my frameworks were. It was like having a brilliant colleague with amnesia.

Then I built a personal AI knowledge system, essentially a persistent context layer that accumulates over time. The short version is: the AI now knows my projects, my priorities, my writing, my positions, my half-formed ideas, even my task list. When I start a session, it already knows what I'm working on and what I've been thinking about.

The difference is hard to overstate. The depth I reach in minute five of a conversation is qualitatively different when there's shared history. I don't re-explain context. I don't re-establish my position. I just... continue thinking. The AI picks up where we left off, connects what I'm saying now to something I wrote three weeks ago, and pushes the thinking forward.

I think there's a progression here that matters, and most people haven't got past the first level:

1. **AI as search replacement**, shallow, overhyped, and honestly the sceptics' target is legitimate here
2. **AI as co-thinker**, stateless but powerful, deepens individual thinking sessions
3. **AI as persistent collaborator**, contextual, cumulative, a fundamentally different kind of relationship

Most people are at level one. A growing number are discovering level two. Almost nobody has experienced level three. The gap between each level is enormous, large enough that they might as well be different technologies.

## Which brings us back to the money

While the comment sections were celebrating Nvidia's dip, I found myself more interested in what the companies with actual skin in the game were doing.

Amazon cut 16,000 corporate roles, 30,000 since October 2025, their largest workforce reduction in 31 years. At the same time, they're guiding $125-200 billion in AI infrastructure spending for 2026. Jassy says it's about "reducing bureaucracy." Perhaps. But you don't cut 30,000 white-collar workers while simultaneously making the largest infrastructure investment in your company's history unless those two decisions are connected at some level.

Alphabet is spending $175-185 billion on AI infrastructure. That's double what they spent in 2025.

These aren't companies hedging their bets. These are companies that can see what the latest models do today, and they're racing to own the platform that knowledge workers will run on tomorrow.

And look at how Anthropic positioned Opus 4.6. This isn't marketed as a better chatbot. It's explicitly a knowledge-work platform, targeting product managers, financial analysts, researchers. Agent teams working in parallel, million-token context windows, Office integration. Claude Code for developers. Cowork for everyone else. They're building the replacement layer for exactly the kind of work that Amazon is shedding headcount from.

The infrastructure race has stopped being speculative. The models can demonstrably do the work. The companies pouring hundreds of billions into capacity aren't guessing. They can see it.

That's why Nvidia bounced back. That's why it will keep bouncing back. The infrastructure layer isn't the bubble. It's the foundation.

## The dot-com comparison is right, but not in the way people think

Every time AI stocks wobble, someone invokes the dot-com bubble. Fair enough, but they usually draw the wrong lesson from it.

The dot-com bust was devastating. Trillions in value evaporated. Pets.com and Webvan and a hundred others went to zero. If you were watching in 2001, it was reasonable to conclude the internet had been overhyped.

But the tsunami only destroyed what wasn't bolted down. What survived, Amazon, Google, the underlying infrastructure, became the foundation of the modern age. The bust didn't prove the internet was a fad. It proved the market had run ahead of reality, and then reality caught up.

The people cheering each AI stock dip as proof the bubble has burst are watching the dot-com tsunami and concluding that the ocean is going away.

## Why I think AI's danger window is closing

The dot-com comparison is useful, but I think AI's trajectory is actually different in an important way.

Launching AI has been like launching a rocket. The first stage requires enormous thrust just to get off the ground, hundreds of billions in capital investment, fundamental breakthroughs in model architecture, the buildout of entirely new infrastructure. That's the stage we've been in. It's expensive, it's noisy, and from a distance it looks like a lot of fuel being burned for not much altitude.

The riskiest moment in any launch is the transition between stages. The first stage fuel is spent, the boosters are jettisoned, and for a brief moment the rocket coasts on momentum alone. If the second stage doesn't fire, the whole thing comes crashing back to earth. That's the scenario the sceptics have been betting on, and six months ago, they had a reasonable case. The gap between investment and demonstrated value was wide enough to make you nervous.

But I think the second stage is firing.

The improvements in reasoning we've seen in models like Opus 4.6, and the knowledge-work use cases those improvements unlock, aren't theoretical any more. They're real, demonstrable, and, as the SaaS stock reaction shows, threatening enough to reprice entire industries. When the market panics not because AI doesn't work but because it works *too well*, that's evidence of thrust, not hot air.

This doesn't mean every AI company will make it to orbit. There will be a Darwinian shakeout, not every startup will survive, not every use case will justify its valuation, and some of today's most hyped ventures will burn up on the way. That's healthy. That's how you separate the bolted-down from the not-bolted-down.

But the trajectory itself? I think we're past the point of maximum danger. The second stage is burning, and it's taking us somewhere real.

## The experience gap

I keep coming back to why the sceptics get this wrong, and I don't think it's because they're unintelligent. I think it's structural.

If your experience of AI is asking ChatGPT to write a birthday message and getting back something generic, of course you think it's overhyped. If you've tried AI image generation and seen the six-fingered hands, of course you're sceptical. If the only AI-generated content you've encountered is the SEO slop clogging up your search results, "who needs this?" is a perfectly reasonable question.

It reminds me of trying AltaVista in 1997 and concluding the internet wasn't going anywhere.

The person using ChatGPT to generate a birthday card and the person using Claude with persistent context as a daily thinking partner are having completely different experiences. They're not even using the same technology in any meaningful sense. And yet the first person feels qualified to declare it a fad, because their experience tells them so.

They're not wrong about what they experienced. They're wrong about what's possible. And that's a distinction worth being precise about.

## What I'm actually paying attention to

The question that interests me isn't whether there will be a market correction. There probably will be. It isn't whether some AI companies will fail. They will. It isn't whether AI generates nonsense sometimes. It does.

The question is whether you can see what's being built underneath the hype. Because the hype will come and go. The hot takes will cycle. The stocks will dip and recover. But underneath all of that, something is changing in how people think, work, and create, and the people experiencing that change aren't the ones writing comment-section hot takes.

The sceptics are right that a lot of what passes for AI usage today is shallow and overhyped. They're just wrong about what that means. It doesn't mean AI is a bubble. It means most people haven't figured out how to use it yet.

That's a very different problem. And it's one that's solving itself faster than the sceptics expect. By the time they notice, the rocket will be in orbit.

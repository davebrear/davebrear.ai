*Why AI is breeding a new kind of intellectual fraud.*

Two decades of social media platforms gave everyone a voice. The last two years of AI gave everyone a content factory. Writing that used to take an afternoon of thinking and an evening of drafting now takes a sentence and a return key. The result is a flood of output with the *shape* of ideas and none of the substance. We even have a name for it: AI slop.

I want to be careful with that name though, because it sweeps up too much.

## The instrument is not the problem

In some circles, AI slop has come to mean any output an AI was anywhere near. That's lazy thinking.

A violin played without skill is grating. That doesn't mean violins all sound like screeching cats. In skilled hands the same instrument makes beautiful music. The instrument isn't the problem. The hands playing it are.

The thing actually worth attacking is much more specific. It's the loop where someone types a short prompt, accepts whatever comes back, and ships it as their own thought. Headings that look like a structured argument. Bullets that look like reasoning. A conclusion that lands with the cadence of one. And underneath, nothing but **Meaning-shaped slop.**

## Writing is a delivery vehicle

Strip writing back to its function and it is transport. A vehicle for ideas to travel from one mind to another. The vehicle's value sits entirely in its cargo.

Think about what you actually do when you write something worth reading. You decide what point you want to make. You frame the problem. You walk the reader through your reasoning, with the routes you considered and rejected baked in. You end with a conclusion you can defend.

Agree or disagree with what's on the page - that cognitive backbone is what makes a piece worth engaging with in the first place. The measure of an idea is the clarity of its reasoning, not necessarily whether it matches your own views.

Slop has none of that. The vehicle is full, but the cargo is not yours. Everyone is 'loading' from the same handful of foundation models, so every driver's cargo looks the same - identical packages from identical warehouses. There is a self-interest argument inside this. Even setting intellectual honesty aside, you are being publicly judged on opinions you have never examined. Not opinions you disagree with, opinions you have never actually tested. Once your name is on it, the public will not make that distinction. You are both exposed and invisible at the same time.

Why is there so much of this? Because a lot of public writing is not on the road to deliver an idea. It is in a parade, painted in team colours, driven where the right people will see it. When being seen by your side is the goal, the cargo only needs to be *recognisable*, not *good* - and AI fills those trailers cheap and fast. The conclusion is settled before the reasoning starts, because the conclusion is the point. Both sides do it.

## Reheated Thinking

The AI slop creation loop needs a name. So I am naming it: **Reheated Thinking**. Here is the metaphor that made the term make sense to me.

An AI model is a freezer full of ready meals. Pre-cooked. Shelf-stable. Engineered for broad palatability. A short prompt is the act of opening the box and putting it in the microwave. Two minutes later, dinner is on the plate.

What comes out is fine. Probably bland. Edible. It is, definitively, no substitute for cooking with your own ideas.

That is the verdict in two parts. **At best, uninteresting to eat. At worst, dishonest to pass off as your own cooking.**

Compression has its place. AI is genuinely good at it - summarising the bloated, automating the tedious, rephrasing the unclear. There are workflows where reheating is exactly the right move. The actual *thinking* though - the deciding, the framing, the reasoning, the conclusion you would defend - that is not a compression task. That is where a human has to be in the kitchen.

## The dinner party test

I don't object to microwave meals in general. Reheating a meal for yourself on a Tuesday night is a personal choice - a convenience that meets your needs.

Serving that same microwave meal at a dinner party is a different thing entirely. Your guests rearranged their evening, drove across town, brought wine. They came expecting something worth the journey. They came expecting *you*. You handed them the microwave instead.

Cook or cater. Both valid. Both statements. Cooking from scratch says you wanted to give them something of your own making. Catering says you wanted to host but made a deliberate choice to bring in someone better placed to do the cooking. Both reveal taste. Both reveal judgement.

The microwave meal says nothing. Anyone can press a button. Pressing a button does not distinguish you from anyone else who pressed the same button on the same machine.

Publication is a dinner party. Every article, every post, every memo, every long reply is a statement about who you are. **Don't serve nothing.**

## In defence of leftovers

That said, I should be careful with how far I stretch the reheating analogy, because I love leftovers.

Sunday's roast becomes Monday's sandwich filling. Friday's pasta becomes Saturday lunch. Reheating in our house is a happy fact of life and a small daily pleasure. If I am going to take a swipe at the microwave, I want to make a clear distinction.

When you reheat **your own cooking**, the remix compounds positively. You know the ingredients. You know what worked the first time. You are applying judgement about what to repurpose and how. The cooking is yours. The reheating is just a second use of the work you already did.

Reusing your own thinking is the same. A note from last year that sharpens a piece you write today. A framework you have built up over months that you apply to a new problem. A turn of phrase that has been knocking around your head for weeks finally finding the right home. That is leftovers. It is good.

Reheated Thinking is the opposite. You did not cook the original. You do not know what is in it. You are not remixing your own work, you are plating someone else's frozen meal and calling it dinner.

## The optimistic turn

I am not down on AI. I use it every day, including to think this piece through. The argument is not that AI is corroding cognition - it is that *Reheated Thinking* is, and AI has made the loop cheap and easy in a way it never was before.

When used well, AI is a lever. It clarifies thinking by forcing you to articulate it. It surfaces connections across your own work that you would have missed. It challenges arguments while you are still building them, when the stakes of being wrong are low and the cost of changing your mind is nothing. It makes the cooking better. It does not make the microwave more available.

Ideas are nutritious. The world is better when real ones are shared. AI can be the thing that crowds them out, or the thing that helps more of them get made.

Remember the violin? The grating beginners are everywhere right now, and they will be for a while. But the instrument is real. In skilled hands it will let us make music we could not make alone: elevated by the tool but still moving, still distinctly human.

That is work worth doing. Next week: the test that tells you whether you are doing it or not.

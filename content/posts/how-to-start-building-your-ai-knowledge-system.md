Several people have asked me how to set up an AI powered knowledge management system. They send me AI-generated infographics like: "8 steps to build your AI workspace." Specific folder structures, specific tools, specific plugins.

Here's the problem with that: if someone hands you a finished system, you'll never understand why it's shaped that way, and you won't know how to change it when your needs do. The people I know who get the most from AI didn't follow a recipe. They started with a problem, talked to an AI, and the system grew from there.

So I'm not going to tell you what to build. But I will tell you how to start.

## Step 1: Give the AI a place to remember you

Pick an AI tool that can read and write files on your computer (Claude Code, ChatGPT Desktop, whatever you prefer). Create a folder. Connect them. That folder is where the AI's knowledge about you will live.

## Step 2: Get interviewed

Don't spend your first session building folders or writing config files. Instead, ask the AI to interview you. Tell it to ask about your work, your pain points, how you capture ideas, what your daily and weekly rhythms look like, and how you prefer to communicate. Let it go deep. When it's done, have it save what it learned to a file in your folder. That file is your system. Your entire system, for now.

## Step 3: Do a real thing

The next day, bring the AI a real task. Not more setup. Prep a meeting. Draft an email. Plan your week. The friction you hit (the things the AI gets wrong, the context it's missing) tells you exactly what to add to your context file. That's how the system grows: from need, not from speculation.

## Three principles I wish someone had told me early

**Principle 1: AI output is raw material, not finished thinking.** The AI gives you something to shape, not something to accept. Keep your thinking and the AI's visibly separate. That distinction matters more than people realise.

**Principle 2: Organise by description, not location.** Resist the urge to create a folder for everything. Describe what things are, not where they sit. A single note can belong to multiple contexts when the description is right.

**Principle 3: Let structure emerge.** Your first month should look messy. If it looks polished in week one, you're spending time on architecture instead of using the thing. When patterns appear, that's when structure earns its place.

The seed matters. The plant is yours to grow.

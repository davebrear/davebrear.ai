*Naming the remedy to Reheated Thinking, and walking the method end to end.*

Last Thursday I named the enemy: **Reheated Thinking**. Opening the freezer, microwaving whatever came out, serving it to your guests, and calling it cooking. Swap the kitchen for the keyboard, and this becomes AI slop where someone types a short prompt, accepts whatever comes back, and ships it as their own thought. AI does not have to be that.

AI can be used instead to deliver augmented cognition. Most of today's AI conversation is about agentic use - workflow automation, agents acting on your behalf - which operates on what Brian Madden calls the visible 20%. **Augmented Cognition** is the other direction: the category of work that amplifies the [invisible 80%](https://www.citrix.com/blogs/2026/01/13/the-invisible-80-what-corporate-led-ai-transformations-cant-see/), the cognition that produces those outputs. In my view this is the more immediately impactful use of AI for knowledge workers. My practice there has been an evolving picture, and I have codified it into the category's signature method. I want to name it here: **Chain of Cognition**.

## Before the method - foundational ideas

I read Sönke Ahrens' *How to Take Smart Notes* a while back and it introduced me to Niklas Luhmann's Zettelkasten - a slip box of index cards, one idea per card, linked by reference numbers. Luhmann used it to publish 70 books and nearly 400 academic articles. Both he and Ahrens are clear about what the box actually is: not a note-taking method, but a thinking system. Go read the book - it changed how I think about note-taking. The method I am naming is a Zettelkasten at its core, with AI as the lever that extends its power in ways a wooden box full of index cards never could.

Before I go into the method I want to introduce three key concepts first:

* **Atom** - or atomic note - a single discrete idea, captured as a short note. The name is literal: an idea is the smallest unit meaning breaks down into, and an atom holds exactly one. One claim, one concept, one distinction. Self-contained - it reads without needing another note to make sense. Concise, usually two to five paragraphs. Written in your voice, first-person where natural. Linked to other atoms where real connections exist.

* **MOC** - Map of Content - a structural note that organises a cluster of atoms around a theme or argument. Continuing the atomic metaphor, MOCs are atoms assembled into molecules to develop a chain of thought. Picture the stereotypical TV-movie corkboard - photographs pinned up, red string connecting them. A constructed narrative of 'an investigation in progress', living alongside the atoms it maps and reusable across multiple pieces.

* **Output** - atomic ideas, assembled via a MOC, transformed into a complete piece of thinking ready to be shared with others.

Atoms and MOCs together make a digital Zettelkasten - a vault, a second brain, call it what you like. Unexpected connections surface naturally as the network grows. Over time it becomes a thinking partner, a conversation with your past self. The thinking accumulates in the links, not in the prose. By the time you sit down to write, the hard work is done.

Many digital tools can hold a vault like this. What matters is the capabilities: linked notes, single-idea granularity, fast traversal, readable by AI as plain text. Any tool that covers all four works.

## Chain of Cognition

Here is the Chain of Cognition method in practice. Four phases, one rule: you do the thinking.

### Phase 1: capture and explore

Phase 1 is where the rule is hardest to follow. The modality you use to capture is wide open. The source of the ideas is not.

Your ideas can come from anywhere. Pen and paper, hand-written notes, typed directly, dictated to an AI, thought through on a walk. The modality does not matter. What has to be yours is the thinking itself.

When you are ready, open an AI chat session. There are two ways to start. Firstly fresh, if there are no prior atoms in play. Otherwise, atom-anchored, if you already have relevant atoms in the vault - point the AI at them, ask it to walk the links, and absorb the full context first. Your prior thinking becomes the session's substrate. Prior work is an asset, not an archive.

The AI's role here is recording rather than generating. Your exact phrasing is the voice signal for everything downstream, so capture is verbatim - no paraphrasing. The AI surfaces adjacent atoms when you ask. It does not draft prose. It does not propose structure. It does not interrupt with clarifying questions - those get held until you signal you are done capturing for the moment.

Phase 1 is cyclical, not linear. You capture seeds. You load context - ask the AI to surface related atoms, walk links, pull clusters into view. You reflect on what surfaced. Does it change what you are thinking? Prompt more capture? Reveal a gap? Then you capture more. No three-step sequence. One loop, running until the atom set for this piece stabilises.

As distinct ideas emerge from the loop, you shape them into atoms. One concept, one file, self-contained, in your voice, linked to related atoms. Build them as you go, or at the end when the raw log is stable. Either works.

Phase 1 ends when the atom set stops wanting edits, you can see the rough clusters, and you are ready to see the shape. The next move is building the map, not more capture.

What tends to go wrong in Phase 1:

* **The AI drafts when it should be recording** - the most common drift. If the AI's output starts looking like prose paragraphs, pull it back and ask for verbatim capture.
* **The AI interrupts with clarifying questions**, trying to be helpful. Useful sometimes. In Phase 1, it breaks flow. Questions should get held.
* **Starting cold when relevant atoms already exist in the vault** - you reinvent what you have already thought through. Load first.
* **Confusing the session note with the atoms themselves.** Session note is raw capture. Atoms are the distilled versions. The distillation step is doing real work.

You leave Phase 1 with three things:

* A session note holding the verbatim raw input log.
* A set of new and existing atoms identified as this piece's substrate.
* Any deepening edits to existing atoms, committed in place.

### Phase 2: the map and the skeleton

Phase 1 gave you atoms. Phase 2 shapes them into a narrative.

Ask the AI to create a MOC for this piece - a note named something like *MOC - [piece title]*, linked to the project and instruct it to stage all the related atoms from Phase 1. The MOC becomes the home for everything from here on. It holds, in this order: a piece summary (one paragraph in your voice, pulled from the raw input log); the atom set (wiki-links to the atoms, grouped by the clusters that emerged from Phase 1); connecting threads (one-line notes on how atoms in different clusters relate); the raw input log itself, transcluded or pasted verbatim; and a running list of structural decisions as you make them. The AI builds the initial structure. You reorder and reshape as you see fit.

Then ask the AI to construct a skeleton from the atom set and the log. Top-level sections with one-line beats per section. After it creates the skeleton, your job is to edit: move things around, delete what does not belong, add what occurs to you - all directly in the skeleton section of the MOC.

Rename sections where the AI's label is not quite right. Merge what it split; split what it merged; reorder; reword a bullet in your own phrasing. Drop bracketed comments where the move is not obvious - *(not sure we need this)*, *(expand this)*, *(is this the right word now?)*. If a bullet is not earning its place, delete it. Prose is expensive; dead bullets are the cheapest thing to cut.

Then ask for a revised proposal. The AI reviews your changes and comments and updates it. Vary the scope - sometimes you mark up the whole skeleton and want the AI to process everything in one pass. Sometimes you only care about one bullet or one section. For me, early revisions tend to be focused - one section at a time, catching specific issues. Later, they tend to be end-to-end read-throughs catching consistency across the whole shape. Work at whatever scope the moment calls for.

If expanding a section reveals content you did not capture in Phase 1, capture it now. Drop back into capture mode, or just append to the raw input log as a Phase-2 extension. Both are fine.

Phase 2 ends when two tests pass:

* Every section has a clear job - not "discuss X" but "make claim Y using atoms A, B, C."
* You can see the shape of the piece without reading any prose. The bullets tell the argument end to end.

What tends to go wrong in Phase 2:

* **The AI reaches for what it knows** - a prior plan, a sibling piece - and the first proposal is that replayed to you. Editing is how you pull it back to what this piece actually wants; the gap between the first proposal and the final skeleton is where the shape of the piece gets decided.
* **Alien sounding framing.** Beats that sound like they came from somewhere else - a template, a prior plan, a sibling piece - not from your own vocabulary. Catch them, examine whether they have merit to you and cut, rephrase or embrace as needed.
* **Wrong word choices at the bullet level.** "Remains" versus "holds" matters already. You do not need to wait for prose to find those.

This phase is where your judgement concentrates. The atoms are raw material; the shape is your taste. An employer or an AI could extract the vault, but the vault is archive, not author. The MOC is a living document. It keeps getting updated through Phase 3 and Phase 4 - new voice signals added, decisions logged, the skeleton revised. It stops being active when the piece ships. Then it becomes part of the vault's permanent record, available to seed future pieces.

### Phase 3: Write it!

Phase 3 turns the skeleton into a full draft. There are two possible ways to do this. Both paths use the skeleton as a map. The difference is who expands the bullets into prose.

* **Path A** - you expand the skeleton into prose yourself, using it as your map. The AI assists with phrasing alternatives, structural feedback, fact-checking.
* **Path B** - you let the AI expand the skeleton into prose instead, using examples of your writing to calibrate tone. Load the inputs first: two to four prior pieces that sound like you, the raw input log from Phase 1 (your exact phrasing before any paraphrase), and any strategic notes the piece carries from the project. Then ruthlessly edit the generated output line by line until it reads as yours.

Path A was my natural inclination when I started this work. Time has shown me that Path B, done correctly with enough quality samples to calibrate against, is indistinguishable from my own typed prose (to me at least!). That is where my head is at now. Path B as the default, Path A when voice stakes are especially high or the sample library is thin.

Path A is also how you seed Path B. Every piece written via Path A becomes a voice sample for future pieces. Early on, Path A is the cold start. You do not have enough samples for Path B yet. Over time those samples compound until Path B is viable. Path A never stops being useful; its role just shifts as the vault matures.

Create a draft file - *[piece title] - Full Draft v1* - linked to the MOC and project. This is where the prose lives. Generate the prose section by section, so editing can begin on completed sections while others are still being drafted.

Then edit what exists. On any non-trivial edit, the AI proposes multiple options - A, B, C - each doing a different thing. It states the trade-off, makes a lean recommendation, defers to you. The answer can often be "none of the above, but B is closest - add this specific beat from A to it and try again." Do not fuse decisions: one edit, one question, one answer; structural and stylistic edits stay separate. When you make a structural call, the AI follows it precisely. "Move section X" means move, not refactor. Re-read before accepting. Hold cuts and additions to the same standard. A line worth protecting from deletion should also be a line you would push back on adding.

And there is the hunt. At section scale, scan for any voice anti-patterns list you keep. Some only become visible when you read the section as a whole, not line by line. Paragraph-labels that announce the argument - "Why this matters", "The key insight", "The takeaway". Hedging openers - "It is worth noting that". Any structure that sounds more like a lecture outline than prose. These pattern-match to AI-generated writing at scale. Your reader will clock them even if they cannot articulate why.

One caveat. AI-isms are a thing because they were based on real writing that followed well established style guides for good communication. If a pattern is genuinely yours, do not drop it just because AI uses it too. I have always bulleted with bolded intros along with hyphens to punctuate points - that does not change now.

Apply two tests to every line. The authenticity test: would I say this? Does this sound like me? Could I defend this in conversation? The AI-voice test: does this sound like every AI-generated piece I have read? Is this a construct I would recognise as an AI-voice tell? The first catches failures of voice. The second catches lines that are plausibly yours in content but pattern-match AI prose in shape. Both matter. If either fails, rewrite or ask for alternatives.

Phase 3 ends when:

* Every line passes both tests.
* The draft covers the full skeleton.
* You read it top to bottom and call it yours.

What tends to go wrong in Phase 3:

* **Starting Path B without samples** - the AI paraphrases the log and produces passable-but-AI-voiced prose. Switch to Path A, which generates the samples you will need.
* **Drifting voice to match AI phrasing** - subtle pressure to accept good-enough lines that are not quite yours. The tests only work if you apply them strictly.
* **AI-voice constructs slipping through** - they sound plausible, pass authenticity, and still betray AI authorship. Keep the anti-patterns list current.
* **Skipping the log as voice anchor** - prior pieces give macro voice; the log gives micro voice for this specific piece; you need both.
* **Upstream thinking that was not solid** - Phase 3 cannot compensate for thin Phase 1 or Phase 2. If the argument fights you at the sentence level, drop back.
* **Fusing structural and stylistic edits** - harder to evaluate either cleanly. Keep them separate.
* **Reversion anxiety** - when the AI re-proposes something you cut, trust the cut. Re-check only if you are unsure the cut was intentional.

If the upstream thinking is solid, the quality of the final piece depends far less on who typed the first sentence than you would expect. The argument is already built. The structure is already tested. The voice is already established in the inputs. Who types, and the exact wording that lands first, are details.

Atoms, MOCs, and outputs make a chain of cognition. That is where the method gets its name. Every link is yours.
